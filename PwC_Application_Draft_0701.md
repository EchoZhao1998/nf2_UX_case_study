## 第三部分 · 2. 项目背景

*(finalized — same n=22 verification)*

### 中文（约305字）

NF2是一种需要长期、多学科管理的罕见遗传病，患者常需辗转不同医院和专科，面临病历难携带易遗失、信息重复整理、沟通成本高等现实问题——这并非个例，而是许多慢性罕见病患者共同面临的挑战。团队预调研（n=22）印证了这一点：100%的受访者未使用任何结构化数字工具管理病史（63.64%依赖纸质资料，36.36%仅靠记忆）；68%在转诊或更换医生时遇到沟通困难，病历零散和问诊时间有限最为常见；91%伴有听力受损或全聋，对医疗信息的可读性与无障碍支持提出更高要求。疾病管理的复杂性与沟通障碍相互叠加，加重患者的决策压力与心理负担。本项目希望通过患者自主的病历管理工具，降低沟通成本，探索更具包容性的医疗信息支持方式。

### English

NF2 is a rare genetic disorder requiring long-term, multidisciplinary management; patients often travel between hospitals and specialists, facing paper records that are hard to carry and easy to lose, repeated re-organizing of the same information, and high communication costs — not an isolated case, but a challenge shared across chronic rare diseases. Our pilot survey (n=22) confirms this: 100% of respondents use no structured digital tool to manage their medical history (63.64% rely on paper records, 36.36% on memory alone); 68% report communication difficulty when referred to a new doctor, most often fragmented records and limited consultation time; 91% also live with hearing loss or profound deafness, raising the bar for readability and accessibility in medical information. These compounding burdens increase patients' decision-making pressure and psychological load. This project aims to reduce communication costs and build a more inclusive standard for medical information support through a patient-led record-management tool.

## 第三部分 · 3. 创新点阐释

*(assigned to a teammate to draft — Echo's notes below, grammar/Chinglish cleaned up only, not restructured or turned into a submission-ready paragraph)*

**Echo's notes**

1. Innovation 1 — Patient-owned Medical Record
Normally, a patient's medical records stay siloed within each hospital's own HIS (health information system). But patients often see doctors across different departments and hospitals — once they see a new doctor, the lack of transparency across these HIS systems reduces communication efficiency between doctor and patient. We want to create an all-in-one medical folder, so the user can collect all their medical reports in one place, and have the folder organize those materials intelligently.

2. Innovation 2 — “Collect First, Organize Later”
Allowing users to simply upload documents while AI automatically extracts, classifies and builds a structured timeline.

3. Innovation 3 — Doctor-ready instead of Patient-ready 
Generate concise, chronological, bilingual(optional), consultation-ready - based on clinical view, what key information doctors need.

4. Innovation 4 — Accessibility by Design 
Accessibility is embedded throughout the design process rather than added afterwards. Team members with hearing, visual and mental disabilities continuously participate in ideation, evaluation and usability testing.

## Competitive benchmark: MyChart (Epic) — for 创新点阐释

Researched 2026-07-01. Use this to sharpen 创新点阐释 — it's stronger urgency evidence than the n=20 survey alone, because it's third-party and already litigated.

**Pros (why it's the incumbent):** one place for labs/meds/immunizations/scheduling, secure provider messaging, fast result alerts. [MyChart Reviews 2026 — SelectHub](https://www.selecthub.com/p/patient-engagement-software/mychart/)

**Cons that map directly onto our problem:**
- **Fragmentation is real and litigated, not hypothetical.** A 2026 federal lawsuit (American Association for Disability Justice + two individual plaintiffs) accuses Epic of fragmenting patient data across separate per-hospital MyChart instances and blocking the record assembly disabled patients need for disability claims — explicitly invoking the ADA and the 21st Century Cures Act's information-blocking provisions. One plaintiff spent 2+ years assembling cross-provider records for a disability claim and became homeless during the process; another's family couldn't retrieve his full record after he died mid-appeal. [Becker's Hospital Review](https://www.beckershospitalreview.com/healthcare-information-technology/ehrs/epic-sued-over-claims-mychart-fragments-patient-medical-records/) · [Nurse.org](https://nurse.org/news/epic-lawsuit-mychart-patient-record-access/)
- **Siloed by default.** Separate account per health system; linking only works if both are on Epic *and* opted into the linking network — doesn't help across countries, or with any non-Epic hospital (most hospitals in China/Malaysia). [Care Everywhere FAQ](https://shareeverywhere.epic.com/FAQ)
- **Geo-blocked outside the US** as an anti-fraud measure; needs a specific VPN to use while traveling, and has only limited footprint in Europe (a few UK/Ireland NHS trusts). This is exactly the access problem Echo hit trying to check it from Malaysia. [MyChart International Access](https://www.mychart.org/Help/international) · [Yale Health — Countries Where MyChart Is Not Accessible](https://yalehealth.yale.edu/topic/countries-where-mychart-not-accessible)
- **Raw data dump, not a summary.** Multiple separate pages per test result, no way to flag what to discuss with a doctor, jargon-heavy notes patients are left to self-interpret. [Folio3 Digital Health](https://digitalhealth.folio3.com/blog/pros-and-cons-of-patient-portals/) · [NWI Journal](https://nwijournal.com/advocating-for-patients-understanding-the-pros-and-cons-of-patient-portal-and-emrs-is-critical-to-better-healthcare-outcomes-wellness-professionals-should-understand-how-to-maximize-their-impact-and/)
- No evidence found of deaf/low-vision/tremor-specific accessibility design; adoption-barrier complaints skew toward older or less tech-literate users generally, not disability-specific accommodation.

**The comparison, in one line:** MyChart assumes one hospital system, one country, one account, and hands patients raw records to interpret alone. Our design is patient-owned (not tied to any single institution's login), works precisely where Epic doesn't operate (Malaysia, mainland China, cross-border care), and outputs a filtered one-page bilingual *summary* instead of a raw dump — the same gap the lawsuit describes, solved from the patient's side instead of waiting on institutional interoperability.
