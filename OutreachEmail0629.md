# Outreach Email 0629

## To Stephen

**Subject:** A self-conducted NF2 project — and a question on longitudinal tracking

Hi Dr. Stephen,

I hope you're well. I still haven't heard back about the research stream, so it looks like I may not be able to join your team? 

Rather than wait, I started a self-directed UX research project based on my own experience with NF2. I'm designing a tool that brings together scattered medical records—MRIs, audiograms, surgeries, and treatments—into a single longitudinal record that patients can easily share with new clinicians.

To inform the design, I ran a patient survey, reviewed the literature, and discussed the concept with a doctor. I deliberately scoped out emotional-support features—daily mood logging, peer support—to keep the MVP focused on consolidating records. But I keep circling back to whether emotion still belongs in the design, just at a different layer: the tone, pacing, and visual language rather than a dedicated feature.

Given your work on peer support and mental health, I'd really value your view on two things. First, where does the line sit between an emotional need that contribute to its own feature and one better served by the tone and design of a functional tool? Second—and this worries me more—are there known ways a records tool can unintentionally heighten anxiety (for instance, surfacing a tumour-growth trend), and what's understood about designing against that?

If it's of interest, I'd be happy to share the survey and a one-page design rationale. Thank you for your time.

Warm regards,  
Echo Zhao
