# NF2 Full-Cycle Disease Management Tool — User Persona Report

*Author: Xuan · Translated to English: 2026-07-11 · Prepared as discussion material for the meeting with Dr Tag Alshehri*
*Source: 用户画像报告_Xuan_0711.pdf (translated from the original Chinese; participant IDs #12, #14, #20, #38 align with the MediPort interview findings report)*

---

## 1. Overview

This report is based on in-depth interviews with 4 NF2 patients and caregivers (including a survey and follow-up probing), combined with the disease characteristics and current care landscape of NF2, to distil three representative user personas. NF2 is an autosomal dominant genetic disorder caused by mutation of the NF2 gene, with an incidence of roughly 1/25,000; there are approximately 42,400 NF2 patients in China. Patients are primarily characterised by bilateral vestibular schwannomas; about 95% become deaf in both ears, and around 70% develop symptoms between the ages of 20 and 40.

The interviews covered different disease stages (<1 year to 10+ years), different age groups (<18 to 56+), and different care roles (patients themselves and family caregivers), giving the sample reasonable representativeness.

## 2. User Personas

### 2.1 Persona 1: Early-stage patient — "The Information-Hungry Explorer"

**Basic information**

- Identity: NF2 patient (aged 18–25)
- Disease stage: <1 year (diagnosed March 2026)
- Location: Mainland China
- Main symptoms: hearing impairment (tending toward complete deafness, highly reliant on captions/text communication), balance disorder

**Representative user story**

Early twenties, sought care March 2026 for declining hearing; a contrast MRI revealed bilateral acoustic neuromas and an NF2 diagnosis. He moved between three hospitals seeking a surgical plan before a Beijing doctor advised "watch and wait." The diagnosis hit hard — he phoned a friend and cried, feeling "the whole world had collapsed," before booking the appointment. In daily life he browses social media, makes video calls, and games — but hearing loss makes calls hard: "I turn the player volume all the way up, and my friend has to show their face so I can read their lips."

**Core needs and pain points**

1. **Information sources are scattered and of questionable credibility.** He searches "NF2" on Xiaohongshu, follows the "Paopao Jiayuan" (泡泡家园) public account, and has read a monograph on acoustic neuroma [mentioned reading a relevant monograph in May this year]. But he admits foreign literature and medical monographs are "too technical, too obscure to understand," and wishes information could be "automatically retold in plain language."

2. **Difficulty organising medical records.** Less than a year after diagnosis, he has already accumulated examination reports and films from several hospitals. When attending appointments he "puts all the materials in one bag and brings it along," then sorts through them on the spot in the waiting room — "if I'm seeing a brain tumour doctor, I only take out the brain tumour films."

3. **Low patient–doctor communication efficiency.** His hearing impairment makes in-person communication especially hard — he cannot hear clearly when the doctor asks "what materials do you have?", his parents accompany him but their relaying is imperfect, and speech-to-text tools are "inaccurate and slow to respond."

4. **Attention to new therapies coexists with anxiety.** He pays particular attention to progress in drug treatments: "recently everyone's saying new clinical drugs might appear — it gives you a sense of hope." But his first reaction after obtaining such information is often anxiety — "after reading it I called my friend, I felt completely devastated."

**Attitude toward the tool**

The patient is open to digital tools: "If a tool or program like this came out, I'd choose to use it first, because this kind of program really is quite a gap in the market." But he is sceptical of AI extraction of information — "I'm very worried it will summarise something different from what the doctor said," especially worried that a scrambled timeline could lead to misdiagnosis. He also raised a key concern: examination results differ across hospitals and across machines, and hearing is also affected by non-disease factors such as colds, rest, and mental stress; if a tool "analyses based on data alone" without accounting for these differences, its credibility is greatly diminished [this point was elaborated in detail in the interview].

*Key needs: plain-language interpretation of cutting-edge literature | cross-hospital timeline integration of medical records*

### 2.2 Persona 2: Experienced patient — "The Calm and Pragmatic Veteran"

**Basic information**

- Identity: NF2 patient (aged 36–45)
- Disease stage: 10+ years (diagnosed 2014)
- Location: Mainland China
- Main symptoms: complete deafness, mild blurred vision
- Care pattern: no fixed hospital for follow-up; decided as needed

**Representative user story**

Diagnosed over ten years ago. Past the anxious phase and now emotionally stable — "I was diagnosed more than ten years ago; that anxious phase is long past." Completely deaf with blurred vision, he's built his own workflow: family assist with communication, AI speech-to-text when needed, doctors type on a phone. He's essentially lost his paper records — "every time I see a doctor I just start a new record book" — and keeps a suitcase of films; but "now the films are uploaded to the cloud, so I just bring the QR code." He's skeptical any reliable therapy arrives within a decade and reads new-drug news as hype to deceive patients or investors.

**Core needs and pain points**

1. **Highly sceptical of new therapies.** He follows NF2 research but believes "a reliable therapy is unlikely to appear within 10 years," and that "the information being hyped right now is either to deceive fellow patients or to deceive investors — not very meaningful." The last time he seriously read related news was about a year ago: "NF2 gene therapy entering the clinical trial stage from 2025 to 2030."

2. **The "invisible barrier" in patient–doctor communication.** He states bluntly that "doctors are unwilling to communicate with me directly," and even with AI speech-to-text tools, "there are still some communication barriers."

3. **Fundamental doubt about the tool's value.** He believes "a tool like this simply won't be competitive" — "purpose-built medical AI will definitely emerge in the future, and your product will have no way to compete with it." He cites "Yinshu" (音书) as an example, noting that "at its core it must also be calling someone else's model, just with some productisation work done on top," and that it "is easily replaced — for instance, phones now commonly have AI caption features, which have gradually replaced this product."

4. **Minimalist information needs.** He is used to introducing his history to a specialist using "a single page" or "a paragraph typed into the phone's notes app" — "just tell the specialist your rough medical history and current main symptoms and that's enough."

**Attitude toward the tool**

Essentially rejecting. "I probably wouldn't use a tool like this at all. If I needed to, I'd just throw it at an AI." He considers a disease-management tool to be "a shell wrapped around a large language model" — "why not just use the large model directly?" But he acknowledges that AI is "very strong at summarising information," and that AI's suggestions "are worth referring to — of course you still have to consult a doctor."

*Key needs (though he himself sees no need for a tool): minimalist consultation summary | cloud imaging integration*

### 2.3 Persona 3: Long-term caregiver — "The Burdened Guardian"

**Basic information**

- Identity: family member/caregiver of an NF2 patient (two interviewees: a mother of a patient aged <18, and a father of a 34-year-old patient)
- Disease stage: 3–5 years (the child) and 10+ years (the 34-year-old patient)
- Location: Mainland China
- Care challenges: the 34-year-old patient has total loss of both vision and hearing, is post-tracheostomy, and bedridden with paralysis

**Representative user story**

"Father Wang" (pseudonym)'s son developed tinnitus in 2015, moved between many hospitals, was diagnosed with NF2 in 2016, and suspended his studies. He successively travelled to multiple hospitals in Beijing and Shanghai, and underwent brain surgery in December 2019. The tracheostomy has remained unclosed since then; from 2017 he lost all vision and hearing, and the family communicates by writing characters on his palm with a finger. He received rehabilitation care locally afterward — "we spent three Spring Festivals in the hospital." Now he is hospitalised every two to three months to manage tracheostomy infections and change tubing — "at least 5–6 hospitalisations a year." The family has bought a suction machine, oxygen machine, and nebuliser, and the parents do rehabilitation with him at home. But he refuses to give up — "his mind is very clear and his will to survive is strong; as long as we are able, we will keep helping him, until we can do no more."

The other interviewee, a mother of a child patient (child aged <18,), 3+ years post-diagnosis. Semi-annual MRI reviews at Xiangya, plus second-opinion trips, each requiring the entire paper archive to be carried and re-explained from scratch. She's active in patient WeChat groups daily — they are her early-warning system and her anxiety source at once. She photographs records but reverts to paper in consults (the album is too scattered), self-compares MRI reports, and skips the doctor when scans look unchanged. When her child's hearing suddenly dropped, she bought group-recommended drugs from the pharmacy to try. She follows drug news closely and can recall specifics the next day.

**Core needs and pain points**

1. **Record-management disaster.** Over ten years they have "travelled all over the country to neurosurgery and cancer/tumour-related Chinese medicine, Western medicine, and folk practitioners," with an MRI every three months and paperwork piling up like a mountain. Phone photo storage runs out — "a lot of them were automatically deleted." Paper films "become blurry over time, like photographs you can no longer see clearly."

2. **Local doctors do not understand NF2.** Back in their home area, "very few doctors know this disease," and county-hospital doctors were "learning as they treated us."

3. **The "time gap" in consultation communication.** Neurosurgeons' "time is precious — they have outpatient clinics, surgeries, and ward rounds," and the caregiver "rambles when explaining back and forth, and the doctor is unwilling to listen."

4. **Reliance on the community for information.** The main information channels are the "Paopao Jiayuan" patient group and doctors on WeChat. On a fellow patient's recommendation he had his son take brigatinib (90 mg/day), "and it felt like a lot of the subcutaneous surface tumours shrank or disappeared." He also actively follows clinical-trial recruitment and once signed up but was not enrolled.

5. **Physical barriers to travelling for care.** Taking his son out "requires bringing a lot of things"; one trial-consultation trip "took a week round-trip and they didn't even get into the clinic before being told he couldn't enrol" — "some of them are unreliable, so I no longer dare to go."

**Attitude toward the tool**

The most enthusiastic adopter. "If the main parts of these illnesses could be organised into one place, it would be much more convenient when going out." He is willing to try an automatic-organising tool, believing "small problems don't matter, the key things are unlikely to go wrong." "Saving it on the phone when going out would be much more convenient, so we don't have to ramble back and forth."

But he is also candid: "I don't know how to use a computer, and there are some things I don't know how to work" — ease of use is the decisive threshold for the tool.

*Key needs: one-tap generation of a consultation summary | multi-dimensional disease timeline (linking symptoms, surgeries, medication, and follow-ups)*

## 3. Core Insights

### 3.1 The "triple bind" of medical-record management

All interviewees face the difficulties of too much paper documentation, unsystematic digitisation, and non-interoperable cross-hospital information. Specifically:

- **Physical burden:** a suitcase full of films [#38]; a document bag stuffed full [#12, #20]
- **Inefficient retrieval:** searching and comparing takes 4–5 minutes [#12]; doctors "don't have time to finish reading the thick history" [#12, #38]
- **Information loss:** insufficient phone storage causing automatic deletion [#14]; films blurring over time [#14]

### 3.2 The "double barrier" of patient–doctor communication

- **Sensory barrier:** hearing impairment means patients cannot clearly hear the doctor's instructions [#20]; vision impairment makes reading materials difficult [#14, #38]
- **Time barrier:** outpatient consultations of under 10 minutes [#12, #38]; doctors "simply don't have time to finish reading the thick history" [#38]

### 3.3 The "trust paradox" of information seeking

Patients and caregivers rely heavily on patient groups for information [#12, #14, #20], but commonly face:

- Psychological burden caused by negative information [#12, #20]
- Cluttered information that is hard to consolidate [#14]
- Unverified and mutually conflicting medical advice [#38]

### 3.4 "Hope and doubt coexist" regarding new therapies

Early-stage patients eagerly follow new-drug progress [#20], while veteran patients hold deep scepticism [#38]. Caregivers sit between the two — actively signing up for clinical trials [#14], while becoming cautious after repeated setbacks [#14].

### 3.5 The "spectrum" of tool acceptance

| User type | Acceptance | Core concern |
|-----------|------------|--------------|
| Caregiver (#14) | ★★★★★ | ease of use, data accuracy |
| Early-stage patient (#20) | ★★★★ | AI credibility, handling of data variability |
| Mother of child patient (#12) | ★★★ | data accuracy |
| Veteran patient (#38) | ★ | product differentiation, competition with general-purpose AI |

### 3.6 The critical "data variability" problem (core insight from #20)

"At different hospitals — even on different machines at the same hospital — the tumour size shown by an MRI is actually different. Hearing tests are also affected by factors like colds, rest, and mental stress. If a program analyses based on data alone, without accounting for these variability factors, the credibility of the trend chart needs to be strengthened."

This is the most critical medical-professional challenge in the tool's design — how to transparently present the limitations and fluctuations of the data to users, rather than offering "false precision."

## 4. Implications for Product Design

Based on the personas above, the product is recommended to focus on the following:

1. **The caregiver (#14) is the core target user.** They have the most urgent need for the tool, are the most willing to try it, and are the most likely to become loyal users. Particular attention must be paid to a minimalist interface and a "zero learning cost" design principle.

2. **Building AI credibility is the key to converting early-stage users.** Users generally say "I'll compare the data myself the first few times, and only trust it once I've used it a few times without errors" [#12, #20]. It is recommended to provide a "human review" mechanism in the early stage, or to clearly label the confidence level of AI-extracted results.

3. **The "data variability" problem must be addressed.** Rather than presenting a single-value trend, the tool should annotate metadata such as the examining institution, equipment, and reporting doctor, and warn that "data across different institutions is for reference only" [#20].

4. **Differentiated positioning must be clear.** The veteran user (#38) points straight to the core — general-purpose large models can also summarise information, so the product's value must be demonstrated through "NF2-specific" depth, not merely by being "a shell wrapped around a large model."

5. **Output format must suit the doctor's context.** Multiple users mention that doctors' time is precious [#12, #14, #38]; a "single-page summary" [#38] and a "PPT-style presentation" [#20] are repeatedly cited as ideal formats. A bilingual function has real value for cross-border consultation [already included in the product concept].

6. **A "filtering and consolidation" function for community information is missing.** Users both rely on the community and suffer greatly from its cluttered information [#12, #14, #20, #38]; if the tool can complement community information (e.g. by consolidating verifiable medical information), it will create unique value.
