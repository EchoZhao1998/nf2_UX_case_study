## Interviewee #12 — Caregiver (mother), patient <18, Mainland China, diagnosed 3–5 yrs

**Survey profile:** hearing loss/fully deaf in family; paper records/folders/film bags; on new symptoms → digs into medical literature; top pain: consult <10 min, doctor can't finish reading thick history; group limitation: incoming stream of negative cases/anxiety is a psychological burden; top-2 features: **AI report parsing + Disease timeline**.

**Q: You're the patient's parent, correct?**
Yes.

**QA1.1: When was your child diagnosed? Where do you do follow-ups now? Have you changed hospitals, and why?**
Found at the end of 2022; surgery at Xiangya. MRI review every six months. (We did change hospitals — see QB1.)

**QA1.2: So consistently at Xiangya. Who handles records and follow-ups at home?**
We keep the films ourselves; the review hospital has records for comparison. His dad is in charge of that — I mostly take the child to the exams; my husband keeps the materials.

**QB1: Please recall your most recent hospital visit — familiar or new doctor? Walk me through how you two prepared the materials, and any communication difficulties.**
Early January, I think. Booked online, doctor ordered the MRI report; after the scan we compared it with the last result ourselves — no change, so we didn't go see the doctor at all. We've booked the next one for July 11. Since we've always done reviews at Xiangya, every time we book the MRI ourselves and compare results ourselves. No change for nearly three years, so no extra doctor visits.
After the 2023 surgery, about six months later, we went to Huashan (Shanghai) and Sun Yat-sen Cancer Center (Guangzhou). For those visits we brought Xiangya's discharge records, diagnosis report, the MRI films — every certificate from every hospital, all of it. And for each new doctor, we took *all* the materials out and went through them once.

**QB2: Have you tried photographing the records into your phone? Ever shown the camera roll to a doctor?**
We do keep photos, but in a face-to-face consult the paper feels more convenient. The photos — because there's so much material — feel very scattered.

**QB3: If I asked you right now for the tumor's size changes over the years, how would you find that answer, and how long would it take?**
At home, I'd pull out the past films and diagnosis results and compare them. Away from home, I'd have to scroll through the phone and compare. Time — maybe 4–5 minutes.

**QC1: When did your child last have a new or worsening symptom? What did you do in the first hour?**
Seven or eight months ago — a sudden hearing drop. I bought a few medicines the group members recommended and tried them. Besides those, I also used an antibacterial ear-cleaning liquid. Within two or three hours it recovered on its own. We stopped the medicine after two days.

**QC2: How did the medication work? Did you check other channels? Did the information reassure you or make you more anxious?**
More reassured, I'd say. Whether or not it works — at least someone has tried it before. It feels like there's hope, something worth trying.

**QD1: Do you follow NF2 research or new therapies? When did you last seriously read related news? What did it say, and what did you do after?**
I follow it often. Most recently — yesterday — I saw a piece on a 公众号 (WeChat official account): Trineumin having the potential to become the world's first NF2 targeted therapy.

**QD2: Are there parts of new information that are hard to understand? Which part interests you most — indications, side effects, trial eligibility?**
What I most want to know: the treatment's indications, side effects, and efficacy.

**QE1: Hypothetical — tomorrow you attend an NF2 charity clinic, 5 minutes with the expert. How would you prepare the records? What do you show first, in what form?**
I'd show the latest MRI film and report, then the post-surgery discharge report.

**QE2.1: If a tool auto-organized all records into a one-page doctor summary, what would you most fear it gets wrong? Which error would make you never use it again?**
What I fear most is it getting the data part wrong.

**QE2.2: If AI extracts the information, would you trust the result?**
A good tool — of course we'd use it. But the first once or twice I'd probably verify the data myself. Only after a few uses with no errors would I relax.

## Interviewee #20 — Patient, 18–25, Mainland China, diagnosed <1 yr

**Survey profile:** hearing loss/fully deaf; paper records/film bags; on new symptoms → quick search on general platforms (Baidu/Zhihu/forums); top pain: scattered MRIs/records, hard to present core progression quickly; group limitation: negative cases/anxiety burden; top-2 features: **AI report parsing + Plain-language research library**.

**QA1: When were you diagnosed? Where do you do follow-ups? Changed hospitals, and why?**
Hearing decline started October 2024; got a hearing aid, no clear improvement. March this year [2026] I had a contrast-enhanced MRI — bilateral acoustic neuromas — and was directly diagnosed with NF2.
There's no fixed review hospital. After the March diagnosis, not knowing the disease well, I first wanted tumor-removal surgery and ran to three hospitals — West China (Chengdu), Huashan (Shanghai), Tiantan (Beijing). The Beijing doctor advised against surgery, so now it's regular observation. I plan to do reviews at whichever hospital is near my work; if I change hospitals, it'll be because I changed jobs.

**QA2: The three things you use your phone for most in a day? Where does your hearing affect operations most?**
Most: (1) video calls with friends, (2) scrolling social-media videos, (3) gaming. Hearing matters most on video calls — I have to turn the playback volume to maximum, and my friends must show their face so we're face-to-face and I can read their lips; only then does communication go reasonably smoothly.

**QB1: Your most recent hospital visit — when, old or new doctor? How did you prepare, and were there communication difficulties?**
June 26–27, Beijing Tiantan — my first time there, so a new doctor. At home we gathered everything from West China and Huashan — hearing tests, contrast MRI films, condition reports — all into one bag and took it to Beijing.
While waiting outside I re-sorted the films: for a brain-tumor doctor, I pulled out just the brain-tumor films, plus the reports showing sizes, into one bag; hearing tests in another bag. The doctor asks for whatever he wants and I hand it over. It's a bit inconvenient though — the doctor mainly wants tumor-size change and hearing-decline change, and checking those one by one is a real hassle.
Communication difficulty — definitely. In a hospital environment, patients like us with hearing impairment can hardly communicate clearly with doctors. For me the problem isn't describing my condition — I'm recently diagnosed, no surgery yet, so describing tumor sizes, locations, hearing decline is manageable. The difficulty is **when the doctor asks me for something** — "show me that exam report", "the hearing test you did at X" — I can't hear what he's asking for. My parents come along but don't really understand this domain, so their relaying is partly difficult. I use real-time speech-to-text, but it has errors and it's slow — the response lags — so communication efficiency is quite low.

**QB2: Tried photographing records into the phone? Shown the album to a doctor?**
I've been to many hospitals, so there are too many record sheets. Storing all the records, reports and cloud films on the phone and showing the doctor — that's complicated too. Scrolling the album might even be slower than flipping through the paper in the bag.

**QB3: If I asked you right now for the tumor size changes over the years, how would you find the answer, and how long?**
At a half-year review rhythm, I'd just recite it: year one, left side this big, right side this big; year two, left…, right…, listing each exam's numbers in order. Said that way in a consult, maybe a minute and a half to two minutes.

**QC1: Your most recent new symptom, and what you did in the first hour?**
The cervical [spinal] neurofibroma is fairly large and affects balance — unsteady walking. Around April, riding a bike, I noticed my balance was off. I suspected the disease but didn't dare think too hard about it. After diagnosis the doctor confirmed the disease affects balance and walking.
In that first hour: I wanted to go to the hospital — is it worse, did the tumor grow, is there a new one — get an MRI. And in between, I asked Doubao [ByteDance AI assistant]: "Is this symptom an NF2 symptom?"

**QC2: Where did you finally get information you considered reliable? Reassuring or anxiety-making?**
Partly from the hospital doctor — this disease does have this symptom. Second, from the patient group — patients share which symptoms occur, and that feels fairly reliable. After getting the information — anxious, definitely. Being ill just makes a person anxious.

**QD1: Do you follow NF2 research? Last time you seriously read about it — what did it say, what did you do after?**
I do follow it — mainly via Xiaohongshu (searching NF2 myself) and the 泡泡家园 (Bubble Home) 公众号 I follow. The first time I *seriously* read about NF2 was in an acoustic-neuroma surgical monograph, around May this year, before my genetic test — though from my symptoms I already knew. The book has 12–14 chapters, almost all on ordinary unilateral acoustic neuroma, with one chapter pulled out for bilateral/NF2: which gene is missing, what symptoms, where fibromas grow, surgical treatment plans, and some newest drug research — but the book is old (2020 or 2022), so its drug-development information isn't very current.
After reading, I basically knew I belonged to this category. The first thing I did was phone my friend — I broke down completely, cried, felt utterly hopeless, like the whole world had collapsed. Only afterwards did I register to ask the doctor.

**QD2: Hard-to-understand parts? Which part interests you most?**
Yes — a lot of drug-therapy information, often recommended in the 泡泡家园 公众号, comes from abroad. Reading those foreign papers, or watching their shared video conferences, feels too obscure — too specialized, especially medical articles and monographs. **I wish it could be automatically retold in plain language.** Most interesting part: treatment methods, especially drugs. Lately everyone — including some doctors — keeps saying new clinical drugs may appear. It hints at hope, so I focus on drug therapies when reading.

**QE1: 5 minutes with an expert tomorrow — how do you prepare, what do you show first, in what form?**
With only 5 minutes, I'd prepare in advance a Word document or PPT: the head and neck tumors are the big ones, so those two locations first. I'd put the cloud-film images into the PPT, annotate size and position next to each, for both head and neck. While the doctor looks, I'd describe my symptoms — my symptoms are fairly easy to describe.

**QE2.1: A tool that auto-organizes all records into one doctor-facing page — what do you most fear it gets wrong; which error means you never use it again?**
Different hospitals describe the condition differently — I don't know whether this summary reproduces them verbatim or the program summarizes on its own.

**QE2.2: If AI extracts the information, would you trust the result?**
If it's program-generated summary, I'd really worry it differs from what the doctors said. And for 1:1 record summarization, I'd fear it **gets the timeline wrong** — e.g., your tumor was X at hospital A, then Y at hospital B; that's a better-or-worse trend, and if it scrambles the dates the trend is completely reversed — **that would make the doctor misdiagnose**. If an error of the misdiagnosis-inducing kind ever appears, I'd never use it again; its reliability would be terrible.
Toward AI extraction I hold some skepticism. That said, if such a tool existed I'd choose to try it first — honestly this category is a blank in the market; we don't even know of such a program. In the first uses I'd double-check its summary and analysis myself; if it holds up a few times, trust increases — trust is a slowly, slowly growing process.

**QS: Anything I didn't ask that we should know?**
Yes. My worry about this kind of tool: program-type tools over-rely on numbers — tumor size, hearing strength — and track your condition into a trend chart. But as far as I know, contrast MRIs done at different hospitals, or even different machines in the same hospital, show *different* tumor sizes. And hearing tests are affected by more than the disease — a cold, poor sleep, stress can all lower results. If the program analyzes on data alone and hands me a not-so-accurate trend chart without accounting for these confounds, the trend's credibility is questionable.
This is a difficulty I've met myself in seeking care: tumor assessment relies more on the doctor's own judgment — is it compressing the brainstem, which tissue is it affecting — not purely on size. Medicine may define 2 cm as a medium tumor, but doctors don't really lean on such hard standards. So purely describing tumor size doesn't seem all that useful; treatment and evaluation rest heavily on doctors' experience.

## Interviewee #14 — Caregiver (father, 56+), patient son 34, Mainland China, 10+ yrs

**Survey profile:** patient has hearing loss/fully deaf + vision impairment + limb weakness/tremor + vertigo/balance issues; **no systematic records — scattered, relies on memory or the doctor**; on new symptoms → record and observe; top pain: scattered MRIs/records; group limitation: information flows too fast, chaotic, hard to search systematically; top-2 features: **Disease timeline + Plain-language research library**.

**Q: You're the caregiver — your relationship to the patient, and his age?**
I should be thanking *you*! Yes, I'm the patient's father. My son is 34 this year (nominal age).

**QA1: When was he diagnosed? Where are follow-ups now? Changed hospitals, and why?**
Tinnitus from 2015; we kept checking and treating it as an ear disease, running to hospitals every winter and summer break. Only in 2016, after he graduated from Zhejiang University and was treating his ears at ZJU Second Hospital during further studies, did a head MRI confirm NF2. He suspended his studies. First Tiantan neurosurgery, then Huashan (Shanghai), Shanghai Ninth Hospital joint clinic, Xuanwu neurosurgery — at least two visits and exams at every one. Finally, on 2019-12-25, brain surgery in Prof. Liu Pinan's ward [Tiantan]. In Nov 2021 we returned to Prof. Liu's ward for the first post-op review.

**QA2: Who manages records and follow-ups? Can the patient participate, and how much?**
He attends the clinic reviews; we parents accompany him. Since mid-September 2017 he has lost **all vision and hearing** — we communicate by taking his finger and writing characters on his palm. After the 2019 surgery he was given a tracheostomy that has never been closed, so post-op he has been in rehabilitation locally ever since.

**QB1: Most recent hospital visit — old or new doctor? How did you prepare; communication difficulties?**
After surgery we came back for rehab in local city- and county-level hospitals — the first 3 years all in Jinhua hospitals; we spent three Spring Festivals in hospital. When things eased and he no longer had to lie flat, the hospital said medical insurance only allows 15-day stays, then you must transfer. After the Nov 2021 [text says 221; also written 22年 later] Tiantan post-op check we came home. Since then, every two-three months we go to the local hospital for infection treatment and to change the tracheostomy and nasal-feeding tubes.
Local doctors rarely know this disease. When we admitted to the county People's Hospital neurosurgery, I told them what I knew — only then did they learn of it. They've been learning while treating us. Now it's mainly trach-infection treatment, exams, tube changes: at least 5–6 hospitalizations a year, each capped at half a month.
At home, we parents do his rehab — we bought a suction machine, oxygen machine, nebulizer; electrotherapy failed; there's been improvement, and improvement overall since before the surgery. The first two-three years post-op he was nearly always in bed; now he hardly lies down, mostly sits in his wheelchair. We do limb rehabilitation and massage using techniques the hospital nurses and doctors taught us. We can't bear to give up — the higher-level hospitals were so conscientious for us, and his mind is clear, his will to live is strong. As long as we still have the ability, we'll keep helping him, until we no longer can.

**QB2: Tried photographing records into the phone? Shown them to doctors?**
Yes — outpatient records and the admission/discharge materials from Tiantan, Xuanwu and others, I'd send to our local doctors. But there were too many; the phone ran out of storage and many were auto-deleted. Most materials are still kept at home. The written records are preserved fairly well, but the MRI/CT films from each hospital — I don't know why — some have become unreadable, blurred with age like old photographs. The most complete is the full-body CT from the military 117 Hospital — nearly ¥7–8k per scan — they gave us a thick book with everything in it. Elsewhere it's discharge/admission summaries and MRI text reports.

**QB3: If asked now for each body part's tumor-size changes over the years, how would you find the answer, and how long?**
For specific numbers I'd have to go dig out materials and compare. When first diagnosed we went practically everywhere in the country — neurosurgery, cancer, tumor specialists, Chinese medicine, Western medicine, even folk practitioners. Every three months a 1.5T MRI, all with reports. Later we learned to ask doctors to annotate each tumor's size and location, and they would. Back then the focus was brain tumors; later exams covered the whole body — MRI, CT, ultrasound — because he has so many everywhere. The largest was groin and cervical/below-ear, subcutaneous, about 9 cm long and 5 cm wide. Now the largest subcutaneous one, at the back of the head, is about the size of a small egg; many more of all sizes on back, legs, feet, head/neck, waist, thoracic spine.
Since [April 2022 — text garbled "20212年4月1号"], on a fellow patient's recommendation, he takes **brigatinib 90 mg daily — never stopped**. We didn't dare go to 180 mg. On 90 mg/day, many subcutaneous/surface tumors seem to have shrunk or disappeared — the visible, touchable ones — so we keep taking it.
But the internal ones — head, cervical/lumbar/thoracic spine — have had **no MRI since the surgery**: Prof. Liu said with the shunt tube and shunt pump in his head, MRI needs [the pump adjusted], and our local hospitals can't adjust a shunt. On the Nov 2022 Tiantan review, Dr. Li adjusted the shunt after the MRI; since then we haven't dared do MRI exams.

**QC1: The last new/worsening symptom — when, and what did you do in the first hour?**
Most recently, [skin peeling], heavy phlegm and saliva — time to change the trach and feeding tubes again. May 15 we went in for admission, exams, treatment, and tube changes.

**QC2: When new symptoms appear, do you ask doctors directly? Other channels? **
Basically, when symptoms appear we head to the county People's Hospital neurosurgery or the county TCM hospital respiratory medicine — after all these years those doctors and nurses know this disease and his condition well, and are very good to us. For understanding what to do: one, ask the doctors; two, our 泡泡家园 patient group on WeChat — we ask patients who've been through it, share with each other, then act accordingly. Some of it is quite effective.

**QD1: Do you follow NF2 research/new therapies? Last serious read — what did it say, what did you do?**
I especially follow the WeChat groups and 泡泡家园's information. I've deleted many of my old army-comrade groups, but every patient group and every disease-related doctor's WeChat I keep. I follow especially 神拓生物 [Shentuo Bio]'s biological therapy, and Tiantan's Zhao/Liu teams researching NF2. Recently I heard Tiantan is recruiting volunteers for a related treatment — **I signed up; don't know if we'll get in.** Last February when Shentuo Bio recruited volunteers I also signed up, but the trial was at the Cancer Hospital (CAMS) with Dr. Wang Shuhang and we couldn't enroll.

**QD2: Hard-to-understand parts? Besides trial eligibility, what interests you — indications, side effects?**
Information posted by better-educated patients and family members I follow closely, but my comprehension is limited sometimes. Between indications and side effects, I mainly watch **indications** — every drug has side effects regardless. I follow clinical-trial matters the most. Our thinking: rather than wait, better to quickly try and rule out. If it happens to work — the drug costs nothing when you need it; and if a trial succeeds, it blazes a trail for fellow patients, and benefits ourselves too.

**QE1: 5 minutes with an expert at an out-of-town charity clinic — how do you prepare, what first, what form?**
Charity clinics — I almost never attend; going out is very hard for us, taking my son out requires so much equipment. If it's about a trial, with the doctor we accept any result — for NF2, having any drug come out is urgent. In recent years we've lost many from our patient groups and my private WeChat contacts.
I once traveled out to the CAMS Cancer Hospital for the Wang Shuhang trial group — a whole week round trip, and at the clinic they said nothing, my son couldn't even enter the consulting room, just "cannot enroll." So some of these aren't reliable, and I don't dare travel anymore.
Sorry for replying late just now — I was suctioning my son's phlegm; it's done now, so I came back to our chat. Hope you'll forgive me.

**QE2: A tool that auto-organizes your son's records into a one-page doctor summary — willing to try? Biggest fear; which error ends it?**
It's been exactly 10 years now. If the main parts could be organized into one piece, going out would be much easier. But — some of this computer work I can't do and don't know how to do. Normally it shouldn't make mistakes, right? Small errors don't matter; the key things probably wouldn't easily go wrong, I think. Summarizing all post-diagnosis records into one article — we'd normally have patients or family look it over for anything to revise, so I don't think it would go wrong. If it can be done, I'm willing to try. Keeping it on the phone when we go out would be so much more convenient — saves us rambling front-to-back while the doctor loses patience. Especially neurosurgeons: their time is precious — clinics, surgeries, ward rounds. Several provincial chiefs have me on WeChat, but I hardly ever disturb them all year.

## Interviewee #38 — Patient, 36–45, Mainland China, 10+ yrs

**Survey profile:** fully deaf + mild vision blur; paper records/film bags; on new symptoms → record and observe; top pain: consult <10 min; group limitation: unverified/conflicting medical advice from group members; top feature: **Plain-language research library**.

**QA1: When were you diagnosed? Fixed review hospital? Changed hospitals, why?**
Diagnosed 2014. No fixed review hospital — most recently the city People's Hospital. Where I review depends on which hospital I go to for care.

**QA2: Three most-used phone activities? You mentioned hearing and vision loss — which operations are most affected?**
Scrolling, reading, communicating. I'm fully deaf, vision mildly blurred; basically no impact on phone operation.

**QB1: Most recent hospital visit — when, old or new doctor? How did you prepare; communication difficulties?**
A month ago; new doctor. My family basically does the talking with doctors. I didn't prepare any records. During ward rounds with the chief I can pull up an AI speech-to-text app, and the doctor can also type to me on a phone. There are still some barriers — **I feel doctors are unwilling to communicate with me directly.**

**QB2.1: Tried photographing records into the phone? Shown the album to a doctor?**
Never. My records are basically all lost. I just open a new records booklet nearly every visit.

**QB2.2: Are your imaging films still kept?**
Yes — a full suitcase by now. Each visit I just bring the most recent films. It's easier now anyway: films go to the cloud, I just bring the QR code.

**QB3: If asked right now for the tumor-size changes over the years — how would you find the answer, how long?**
From memory. One second.

**QC1: Most recent new/worsening symptom — when, and the first hour?**
About half a year ago. Did nothing — dragged it out until a month ago, then got the exam. Result: nothing life-threatening yet, so I've left it. When I finish what's on my plate, I plan to get surgery.

**QC2: On new symptoms, do you consult doctors, search online, check patient groups? Does the information calm you or worsen anxiety?**
I search for information; when I get the exam and after, of course I consult the doctor. My mood is stable — no feelings, no anxiety. It's been more than ten years since diagnosis; the anxiety phase passed long ago.

**QD1: Last serious read of NF2 research/therapy news — when, content, what did you do after?**
About a year ago. Gist: NF2 gene therapy entering clinical-trial phase between 2025 and 2030. I remember I introduced that article on **my own 公众号**.

**QD2: Hard-to-understand parts? What interests you most?**
I currently pay little attention to new therapies — I estimate no reliable therapy within 10 years. The information out there is either to scam patients or to scam investors. Not much point.

**QE1: 5 minutes with an expert tomorrow — how do you prepare, what first, what form?**
I once wrote a single page for an expert. Now I'd probably type a paragraph in my phone's memo app: rough disease history, current main symptoms. That's enough.

**QE2.1: A tool that auto-organizes all your records into a one-page doctor summary — biggest fear; which error ends it?**
**I simply wouldn't use such a tool. If I need that, I just throw it all at an AI directly.**

**QE2.2: Would you trust AI-generated summaries and recommendations?**
Didn't you say "summary" before? AI's summarization ability is strong. Where did "recommendations" suddenly come from? AI recommendations I'd only treat as reference — of course I'd still consult doctors. That tool you describe, as I understand it, is a shell wrapped around a large language model — so why wouldn't I just use the LLM directly?

**QS: Anything I didn't ask that we should know?**
I think such a tool would have no competitiveness at all. Purpose-built medical AI will certainly come, and your product can't compete with that. 音书 [Yinshu] made a communication phone for deaf people — underneath, surely also calling someone else's model; they only did some productization. Products like that are easily replaced — now that phones commonly have built-in AI captions, that product is gradually being replaced.
