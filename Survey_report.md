# MediPort User Research Report

## Phase 1 — Quantitative Survey Findings

> **Project**
>
> **MediPort** is a UX concept that explores how digital tools can better support the long-term self-management of Neurofibromatosis Type 2 (NF2). This report summarizes findings from the initial quantitative survey and identifies research questions for the subsequent interview phase.

---

# Executive Summary

## Research Objective

Living with NF2 requires continuous management across multiple years, hospitals, and healthcare providers. Patients and caregivers must repeatedly organize MRI reports, surgical histories, hearing assessments, and treatment records while making decisions under uncertainty.

To understand existing behaviours before proposing solutions, a quantitative survey was conducted to explore:

- how patients currently manage longitudinal medical information;
- how they communicate with healthcare providers;
- how they seek trustworthy information; and
- which digital support features they perceive as most valuable.

Rather than validating a predetermined solution, this survey aimed to identify behavioural patterns, unmet needs, and opportunities for user-centred design.

---

## Methodology

**Research method**

Online questionnaire

**Participants**

38 valid responses

**Recruitment**

Chinese NF2 WeChat community

**Survey duration**

June 2026

---

## Key Findings

The survey identified four recurring themes:

1. Medical information remains highly fragmented throughout the patient journey.
2. Accessibility requirements are fundamental rather than optional.
3. Patients value trustworthy interpretation more than raw medical information.
4. Longitudinal communication with healthcare providers remains inefficient.

These findings support the need for a patient-led care management solution while also revealing several behavioural questions that require qualitative investigation.

# Findings

## Finding 1 — Longitudinal medical records remain fragmented

### Evidence

Most respondents still rely on physical paperwork or scattered records rather than dedicated digital tools for tracking MRIs, surgeries, and disease progression.

### Interpretation

Existing healthcare systems effectively store individual clinical encounters but provide limited support for managing an entire lifelong disease journey. Patients and caregivers therefore assume responsibility for consolidating information themselves.

### Design implication

Rather than replacing hospital systems, MediPort should function as a portable longitudinal record that integrates information across multiple providers.

---

## Finding 2 — Accessibility is a baseline requirement

### Evidence

Hearing impairment was the most frequently reported accessibility challenge. Many respondents also experienced combinations of hearing, vision, balance, and motor impairments.

### Interpretation

Accessibility cannot be treated as an optional preference. Progressive functional decline directly influences everyday interaction with digital products.

### Design implication

Accessibility should become a core design principle throughout the product, including typography, interaction size, visual hierarchy, motion design, and communication methods.

---

## Finding 3 — Patients seek trusted interpretation rather than raw information

### Evidence

When experiencing a new symptom, respondents most commonly preferred contacting physicians, consulting patient communities, or searching online.

However, when prioritising future product features, the Plain-Language Research Library received the highest number of selections.

### Interpretation

At first glance these findings appear contradictory.

The survey suggests that patients are not necessarily interested in reading medical literature themselves. Instead, they value rapid access to trustworthy interpretation.

During urgent situations, users prioritise reassurance over independent research. Outside acute episodes, they still wish to understand emerging treatments and clinical evidence, but expect that information to be translated into practical, patient-friendly language.

### Design implication

Future product concepts should focus on evidence interpretation rather than simply providing access to research papers.

---

## Finding 4 — Communication challenges stem from fragmented information rather than lack of medical knowledge

### Evidence

Respondents identified fragmented historical records and limited consultation time as the primary barriers when visiting new specialists.

### Interpretation

The communication burden arises from assembling and explaining years of distributed medical history within a short consultation rather than understanding individual reports.

### Design implication

Supporting rapid information handover should become a primary design objective for the MVP.

# Limitations

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting these findings.

## Participant composition

The survey was distributed through an NF2 caregiver WeChat community. Although both patients and caregivers participated, caregivers were slightly overrepresented.

Because caregivers frequently coordinate appointments, organise medical records, and communicate with healthcare providers, their perspectives remain highly relevant to a patient-led care management system. However, findings relating to symptom-response behaviour may not fully represent independently living patients.

---

## Sample size

The survey represents an exploratory study within a rare disease community and should not be interpreted as statistically representative of the wider NF2 population.

Instead, its purpose is to identify behavioural patterns that inform subsequent qualitative research.

---

# Remaining Questions

While the survey successfully identifies several behavioural patterns, quantitative data alone cannot explain why these behaviours occur.

The following questions emerged as priorities for qualitative interviews.

| Survey finding | Remaining question | Interview objective |
|----------------|-------------------|--------------------|
| Most respondents rely on paper records | Why have digital alternatives not been adopted? | Understand current workflow and barriers |
| Research Library ranked highest | What does "understanding research" actually mean? | Explore users' mental models and expectations |
| Doctors are the first response to new symptoms | How do patients decide when professional care becomes necessary? | Understand real-world decision making |
| Medical history is fragmented | Which part of organising medical records creates the greatest burden? | Identify the highest-value workflow to support |
| Consultation time is limited | Which information is most difficult to communicate? | Inform the design of a doctor-ready summary |

---

# Next Phase

To answer these questions, the next stage of the project will conduct semi-structured interviews with approximately 3–5 participants.

Rather than evaluating interface designs, the interviews will explore:

- longitudinal disease management workflows;
- medical record organisation behaviours;
- healthcare communication experiences;
- trust in medical information;
- symptom-related decision making.

Insights generated from these interviews will directly inform the development of personas, journey maps, problem statements, and product design requirements for MediPort.
