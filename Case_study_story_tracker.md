### M00 — Recruiting Xuan as clinical stakeholder   (raw)

- About six months ago Xuan messaged me feeling depressed and aimless about her future, and shared her PhD plans (NF2 research). I noted it.
- When the case-study idea formed, I recognized the right moment to invite her in for the clinical perspective my own patient view couldn't cover.
- **Interview angle:** turning a personal relationship + good timing into a cross-disciplinary collaboration; reading a stakeholder's motivation.
- *To polish: the empathy + timing read. Keep it honest, not transactional.*

### M01 — Designing the survey with a critical mindset   (raw)

- Constraint: in mainland China I could only reach DeepSeek smoothly — my first AI tool for this. It generated a survey in seconds that *looked* fine; I lightly edited and dropped it into Notion.
- Decision: on review I asked of each question — *how does an answer here change a design decision? what trend would it imply?* By that test, the AI draft scored low: plausible-looking but generic, "questions because surveys ask these." I rebuilt around "begin with the end."
- **Interview angle:** using AI as a fast draft but applying a critical filter — judging output by whether it drives a decision, not whether it looks complete. Strong differentiator; feature this in the case study's Research section.
- *To polish: tighten language, native phrasing.*

### M02 — Networking

**Situation**
- I needed to publish the survey in the right groups to reach real candidates.
- I know first-hand data collected from real people matters more than anything else here.
- I'm someone who "hates" time-consuming, fragmented back-and-forth with people. But I'm determined — I'll push to hit the goal within whatever tolerance I can manage.
- The people themselves: rebarbative.

**Task**
Collect at least 30 survey responses.

**Action**
- Publish
    - In the China market, the best candidate pool is WeChat NF2 patient groups. There's a group I'm already in with close to 500 people. I reached out to the admin and explained my intention — he rejected me immediately. I didn't give up: I explained my identity and the purpose of the survey clearly, but casually rather than as a formal proposal, to signal I understood the group's culture. He eventually let me into a smaller group of around 200 people, which turned out to be much less active — a lower-value community, I assumed.

    A day passed with too few responses to understand the real pain points or decide on core features. I started looking for another channel. I DM'd a friend who works at an Autism NGO; she recommended recruiting through Rednote (Xiaohongshu), a channel I'd initially ruled out because it's strict about QR codes and third-party links. She told me I could post plain text instead — something like "recruiting XXX..." with a call to action to DM in the comments — and sent me an example.
    I acted on it immediately. Alongside that post, I searched the keyword "NF2," DM'd users who'd made relevant posts, and joined another NF2 group on Rednote.

    The next day, responses were still thin. The most active group — the 500-person one — still didn't allow me to post the survey link directly. So I tried another approach: I posted a note, Rednote-style, asking people willing to fill out the survey to add me as a friend so I could DM them the link directly.

**Result**
Hit my n=30 goal in one day.

**Learning**
- Effective communication — reading each community's norms and adapting the ask, rather than using one pitch everywhere.
