**Subject: Exploring Patient-Centred NF2 Research – Introduction and Request for Guidance**

Dear Children’s Tumour Foundation Australia Team,

I hope this email finds you well.

My name is Wanting (Echo) Zhao, and I am currently a Master of Data Science student at Monash University Malaysia. Although I study at the Malaysia campus, I am pursuing a Monash University degree and hope to contribute to the broader NF2 community across the region.

Over the past year, my condition has changed significantly. Following surgery, I have completely lost hearing in both ears, developed bilateral facial paralysis, and continue to experience balance difficulties. More recently, I have also become increasingly concerned about worsening vision and muscle weakness. Like many people living with NF2, I have learned that the disease is not only about tumours or clinical treatments—it also changes how we communicate with healthcare professionals, navigate everyday life, make decisions under uncertainty, and cope with the emotional impact of a lifelong condition.

Perhaps because of these experiences, I have become increasingly interested in whether I could contribute to improving the lived experience of people with NF2 through research.

During my recent academic break, I began independently exploring this question through a small UX research case study. Although it is only an exploratory project and based on a very small number of participants, it has allowed me to start learning qualitative research methods, literature synthesis, and user-centred design. Alongside this work, I have been reviewing published literature on NF2, particularly around patient experience, communication, peer support, mental wellbeing, and healthcare interactions.

While reading through the Children’s Tumour Foundation Australia’s website and research materials, I was genuinely impressed by the Foundation’s commitment to advancing NF research and supporting the community. I also carefully read your recent NF Research Report, which helped me better understand both the breadth of ongoing research and the Foundation’s long-term vision.

Reading these resources also prompted a reflection. Much of the current research understandably focuses on genetics, biology, diagnosis, and treatment—areas that are essential and life-changing. However, through both the literature and my own experiences, I have become increasingly interested in another complementary perspective: understanding how people with NF2 communicate complex and lifelong health journeys, manage uncertainty, access peer support, and maintain quality of life throughout different stages of the disease.

From my literature review so far, these areas appear to be comparatively less represented than biomedical research, despite their substantial impact on daily life. My preliminary UX interviews have also suggested that communication challenges—particularly during specialist consultations and across fragmented healthcare journeys—may represent an important area deserving further investigation. While these observations are still preliminary, they have strengthened my motivation to develop this direction through rigorous academic research in partnership with the community.

I am currently preparing to undertake a minor research thesis at Monash University, where I hope to develop a patient-centred research project related to NF2. My goal is not simply to study patients, but to work with the community to identify research questions that genuinely reflect patients’ priorities and unmet needs.

Before progressing further, I wanted to reach out to your organisation because I believe the perspectives of patient organisations are invaluable in shaping meaningful research. Rather than assuming I already know what the community needs most, I would greatly appreciate the opportunity to learn from your experience.

If someone within your team would be willing to share their perspectives, I would be extremely grateful for a brief conversation about:

* what unmet needs you believe currently deserve more research attention for people living with NF2;
* whether patient-centred research on communication, care journeys, peer support, or quality of life aligns with priorities you have observed within the community; and
* any advice you may have for a student researcher hoping to conduct meaningful and responsible research in this area.

If it would be helpful, I would be pleased to share a brief summary of my exploratory UX case study. Although it is still an early-stage project completed independently during my university break, I hope it demonstrates my commitment to understanding the community before attempting to propose solutions.

As someone living with NF2 myself, I know that I cannot change the disease. However, I hope that by combining my lived experience with formal research training, I may eventually contribute—even in a small way—to making future healthcare experiences better for others facing the same journey.

Thank you very much for taking the time to read my email. Regardless of whether there is an opportunity to connect, I sincerely appreciate the work the Children’s Tumour Foundation Australia continues to do for people and families affected by NF.

If there is someone else within the Foundation who would be a more appropriate person to speak with regarding research or community engagement, I would be very grateful if you could kindly forward my email.

I look forward to hearing from you.
