I have NF2, a rare disease that requires lifelong MRI monitoring. Last week I asked my own patient community how they manage a lifetime of medical records.

38 people answered. Not one of them uses a structured digital tool. Not one.

Two-thirds carry paper folders and imaging films from hospital to hospital. The rest rely on memory. And 71% say this breaks down exactly where it matters most — in front of a new doctor, with 10 minutes on the clock.

The finding that surprised me most wasn't any of those numbers. It was the gap between what people *say* they want and what they *do*: half picked a "plain-language research library" as a must-have feature, but only 8% actually consult research when a new symptom appears. Stated preference favors features we imagine using. Behavior points at the rare, high-stakes moment instead.

Honest caveat: n=38 is directional, not statistical. But in a 1-in-33,000 disease, 38 respondents — and 30 interview volunteers — is the kind of community access most research teams never get. That's the next step: in-depth interviews, then a concept prototype (a patient-owned disease timeline that generates a doctor-ready one-page summary).

Full snapshot in the carousel below. If you work in accessibility, health UX, or rare-disease tech, I'd love to compare notes — DMs open.
